Do You Qualify for the Small Estate Process?

New York's Voluntary Administration process under SCPA §1310 is only available for estates that meet all three qualifying conditions. If any one of these conditions is not met, you need full probate or administration instead.

✓ You Qualify If:

  • Total personal property is under $50,000
  • No real estate solely in the decedent's name
  • No known lawsuit or cause of action
  • All three conditions above are met

✗ You Do NOT Qualify If:

  • Personal property exceeds $50,000
  • There is real estate in the decedent's name alone
  • There is a possible wrongful death or other lawsuit
  • Any one of the disqualifying conditions applies

Important: Joint bank accounts, life insurance payable to a named beneficiary, and retirement accounts with named beneficiaries typically do NOT count toward the $50,000 threshold — they pass outside the estate. Only assets that would pass through the estate count. When in doubt, use the free inheritance tool to confirm.

What the NY Small Estate Affidavit Actually Is

The official name is the Affidavit in Relation to Voluntary Administration. It is filed in New York Surrogate's Court — the same court as full probate and administration, just with a simpler process.

The voluntary administrator (the person filing) swears under oath to the court that the estate qualifies, lists all heirs, and requests authority to collect and distribute the estate's personal property. Once approved, you receive Letters of Voluntary Administration — the legal document that lets you access bank accounts, collect assets, and distribute them to heirs.

🚨 Most Common Reasons Small Estate Filings Get Rejected

  • Incorrectly including joint assets or beneficiary accounts in the $50,000 calculation
  • Failing to disclose real estate that was in the decedent's name — even if they owned it with others
  • Incomplete Distributee Summary — missing grandchildren of a deceased child
  • Filing in the wrong county — must be where the decedent was domiciled
  • Not notarizing the affidavit — the Affidavit in Relation to Voluntary Administration MUST be notarized
  • Using full probate or administration forms when filing a small estate — wrong document set entirely

Documents Required for NY Small Estate Filing

1
Affidavit in Relation to Voluntary Administration

The main document. You swear under oath that the estate qualifies, confirm no real estate in the decedent's name, list all distributees, and describe the estate assets. Must be notarized — sign in front of a notary, not before.

2
Certified Death Certificate

An official certified copy from the vital records office — not a photocopy. Order at least 2 copies in case the court keeps one.

3
Family Tree / Distributee Summary

Lists every heir with their name, relationship, address, and whether they are living. Start with the closest relatives first: spouse, then children (including children of any deceased child), then parents if no spouse or children, then siblings. Do not leave anyone out.

4
Court Filing Fee

Surrogate's Court filing fees for voluntary administration are lower than for full probate or administration but vary by county. Confirm the exact fee with the clerk before arriving.

How to File — Step by Step

1
Prepare your Affidavit and Distributee Summary

Fill in all fields completely. List every heir. Do not leave Social Security fields blank — write NONE if one doesn't exist.

2
Notarize the Affidavit

Bring the unsigned affidavit to a notary. Sign in front of the notary. Bring valid photo ID. The notary stamps and signs after witnessing your signature.

3
Go to Surrogate's Court in the correct county

File in the county where the decedent lived — not where they owned property or where you live. Bring the original notarized affidavit, certified death certificate, distributee summary, and filing fee.

4
Receive Letters of Voluntary Administration

Once approved, the court issues Letters of Voluntary Administration. Use these to access bank accounts, collect assets from financial institutions, and distribute the estate to heirs.
