Chapter 7 vs Chapter 13 Bankruptcy

Chapter 7 bankruptcy is a liquidation bankruptcy — a trustee sells your non-exempt assets and uses the proceeds to pay creditors. Most remaining debts are discharged (eliminated) within 3–6 months. You must pass a means test based on your income to qualify. Chapter 13 is a reorganization bankruptcy — you keep your assets but repay a portion of your debts through a 3–5 year court-approved repayment plan. Chapter 13 is often used to save a home from foreclosure.

The Automatic Stay

Filing for bankruptcy immediately triggers an automatic stay — a court order that stops virtually all collection activity. The automatic stay stops: debt collection calls and letters; pending lawsuits; wage garnishments; bank levies; foreclosure proceedings; and utility shutoffs. The stay goes into effect the moment you file, before you even notify creditors.

New York Bankruptcy Exemptions

New York allows debtors to choose between federal exemptions and New York State exemptions. Key New York exemptions include: homestead exemption up to $89,975 (varies by county); motor vehicle up to $4,550; household goods and furnishings up to $11,375; retirement accounts (most are fully exempt); wages owed up to 90% or 30 times the minimum wage; and the wildcard exemption of up to $1,150 plus unused homestead exemption.

Effect on Debt Lawsuits

If you file for bankruptcy before a default judgment is entered, the automatic stay stops the lawsuit immediately. If a judgment was already entered, bankruptcy can discharge the underlying debt, but the judgment lien on real property may survive unless you take additional steps to avoid the lien. If a debt lawsuit is pending against you, bankruptcy may be one option — but filing a written answer to the lawsuit first may also resolve the situation without bankruptcy.
