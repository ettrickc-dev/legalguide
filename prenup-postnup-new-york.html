What Is a Prenuptial Agreement?

A prenuptial agreement (prenup) is a contract signed by two people before marriage that defines each person's financial rights and responsibilities during the marriage and in the event of divorce or death. In New York, prenups are governed by Domestic Relations Law Section 236(B)(3) and are enforceable if properly executed.

What Can a Prenup Cover?

  • Division of property owned before marriage (separate property)
  • Division of property acquired during marriage (marital property)
  • Spousal support (alimony) — amount, duration, or waiver
  • Protection of business interests
  • Protection of children from prior relationships
  • Treatment of debts brought into the marriage
  • Inheritance rights and estate planning coordination

What a Prenup Cannot Cover

New York law prohibits prenups from: determining child custody or child support; waiving rights to public assistance; encouraging divorce; or including any provision that violates public policy or is unconscionable. Any provision limiting child support will be unenforceable.

Legal Requirements in New York

For a prenup to be valid in New York, it must be in writing, signed by both parties, and acknowledged before a notary public. Both parties should have the opportunity to consult with independent counsel. Courts may invalidate a prenup if it was signed under duress, without adequate disclosure of assets, or if one party did not understand what they were signing.

What Is a Postnuptial Agreement?

A postnuptial agreement (postnup) is essentially a prenup signed after marriage. They are valid in New York under the same requirements as prenups but are subject to closer scrutiny by courts since the parties are already in a committed relationship. Postnups can be useful when circumstances change significantly after marriage — such as a new business, inheritance, or change in financial situation.
