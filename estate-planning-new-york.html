The Essential New York Estate Planning Documents

A complete NY estate plan typically includes four core documents: a Last Will and Testament, a Statutory Short Form Power of Attorney, a Health Care Proxy, and a Living Will (Advance Directive). Together, these documents cover your financial affairs, medical decisions, and the distribution of your assets after death.

Power of Attorney — Your Most Urgent Document

The NY Statutory Short Form Power of Attorney authorizes your chosen agent to manage your financial and legal affairs if you become incapacitated. Without it, your family must petition the court for guardianship — a process costing $5,000–$10,000 or more. Health Care Proxy

A Health Care Proxy designates someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if you cannot make them yourself. This is different from a POA, which covers financial matters only. Your agent cannot make health care decisions under a standard POA — you need a separate Health Care Proxy for that.

Last Will and Testament

A will directs how your assets are distributed after death and names a guardian for minor children. Without a will, New York's intestacy laws determine who inherits your property — which may not match your wishes. Estates without a will must go through probate, which is a public court process.

Probate in New York

Probate is the court process of validating a will and distributing a deceased person's estate. In New York, this is handled by the Surrogate's Court in the county where the deceased lived. Probate can take 9 months to 2 years and typically costs 3–7% of the estate's value in legal fees.
