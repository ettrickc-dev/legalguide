Which Court Handles Debt Cases in New York City?

Most consumer debt lawsuits in New York City are filed in the Civil Court of the City of New York. This court has jurisdiction over cases involving up to 5,000. Cases are heard in the borough where the defendant lives — Kings (Brooklyn), Queens, New York (Manhattan), Bronx, or Richmond (Staten Island).

What Do You Need to File?

  • Your completed written answer form
  • A copy for yourself
  • A copy for the plaintiff's attorney
  • Proof of service (showing you mailed a copy to the plaintiff's attorney before filing)

Where Do You File the Answer?

File your answer at the Civil Court clerk's office in the county listed on your summons. Bring your completed answer, proof of service, and your index number. There is generally no filing fee for filing an answer as a defendant.

What Happens After You File?

The court will schedule the case for a hearing date. Both sides appear, the plaintiff must present their evidence, and you present your defenses. Many cases resolve at the first appearance — through dismissal, settlement, or adjournment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if I don't file an answer?
The plaintiff will apply for a default judgment. Once granted, they can freeze your bank account, garnish up to 10% of your gross wages, and report the judgment to credit bureaus — all without any further court hearing.
How long do I have to file an answer in New York?
Generally 20 days if you were personally served, or 30 days if served by other methods. Check your summons for the exact deadline — the clock starts from the date you were served.
Do I need a lawyer to file an answer?
No. New York allows you to represent yourself (pro se) in civil court. The written answer form is designed for self-represented defendants. Our free tool walks you through every field.
What if the debt isn't mine or the amount is wrong?
These are valid defenses. Check "identity theft / mistaken identity" or "I dispute the amount of the debt" on your answer form. Filing an answer forces the plaintiff to prove their case in court.
