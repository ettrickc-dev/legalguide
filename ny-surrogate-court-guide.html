What Is the Surrogate's Court?

The Surrogate's Court is a specialized New York court that has exclusive jurisdiction over matters relating to the affairs of decedents (deceased persons). Every county in New York has its own Surrogate's Court. The court handles probate of wills, administration of intestate estates (when there is no will), and certain guardianship matters.

Probate — When You Have a Will

Probate is the court process of validating a deceased person's will and authorizing an executor to distribute the estate. To begin probate in New York, the executor files a petition with the Surrogate's Court in the county where the decedent lived. The court issues letters testamentary, authorizing the executor to act. Creditors must be notified and paid before assets are distributed to beneficiaries.

Administration — When There Is No Will

If a person dies without a valid will (intestate), the Surrogate's Court appoints an administrator to manage the estate. New York's intestacy laws determine who inherits — generally a surviving spouse, children, parents, or siblings in that order. The process is similar to probate but can take longer due to disputes about who has the right to administer.

How Long Does NY Probate Take?

Simple, uncontested estates in New York typically take 9 to 18 months to complete probate. Complex estates, or those with disputes, can take several years. The cost of probate — including attorney fees, court fees, and executor commissions — typically ranges from 3–7% of the estate's gross value.

Small Estate Procedure

If the decedent's total probate estate is worth $50,000 or less (excluding real property), New York's Voluntary Administration procedure allows the estate to be administered without full probate. This is significantly faster and less expensive than regular probate.
