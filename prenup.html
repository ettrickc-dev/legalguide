Do You Actually Need a Prenup?

A prenuptial agreement is not just for wealthy people. It protects anyone who enters a marriage with assets they want to keep separate — a business, an inheritance, real estate, retirement savings, or debt they don't want their spouse to be responsible for.

Without a prenup, New York's equitable distribution law applies to your divorce. That means a court decides what's fair — and "fair" doesn't always mean what you'd expect.

What a Prenup Can and Cannot Do in New York

What it CAN do:

  • Keep pre-marital assets (property you owned before the marriage) separate
  • Protect a business you own from being divided in a divorce
  • Specify what happens to an inheritance or gift received during marriage
  • Limit or define spousal support (maintenance) obligations
  • Protect each party from the other's pre-existing debts
  • Clarify ownership of property acquired during the marriage

What it CANNOT do:

  • Waive child support rights (courts will not enforce this)
  • Determine custody or visitation of future children
  • Include anything that encourages divorce (courts may void such clauses)
  • Be signed under duress or without full financial disclosure

What New York Requires for a Prenup to Be Enforceable

New York has strict requirements under DRL §236B(3). For a prenuptial agreement to hold up in court:

  • Must be in writing — oral prenups are not enforceable
  • Must be signed before the marriage
  • Must be acknowledged before a notary (same formality as a deed) — not just witnessed, but properly acknowledged
  • Full financial disclosure — both parties must have a fair understanding of the other's financial situation
  • No duress — presenting a prenup the night before the wedding is a red flag courts take seriously

Prenup vs. Postnup — What's the Difference?

A prenuptial agreement is signed before the marriage. A postnuptial agreement is signed after you're already married. Both are valid in New York and serve similar purposes.

Postnups are commonly used when circumstances change after marriage — one spouse starts a business, receives a significant inheritance, or the couple simply realizes they should have had a prenup but didn't.

New York courts scrutinize postnups slightly more carefully than prenups, since the power dynamics can differ once parties are already married. Proper structure and full disclosure are especially important.
