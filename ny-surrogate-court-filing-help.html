First: Which Path Do You Need?

Everything in NY Surrogate's Court starts with one question: did the person who died leave a will? Your answer determines which of the three paths applies.

📜
Probate
Use when: there IS a will. The will must be proven valid before anyone can act on it.
⚖️
Administration
Use when: there is NO will. A family member petitions for authority to handle the estate.
📋
Small Estate
Use when: personal property is under $50K AND no solo real estate AND no lawsuit.

🚨 Most Common Reasons Surrogate's Court Filings Get Rejected

  • Filing for probate without the ORIGINAL will — photocopies are never accepted
  • Missing or incorrect heir information in the Distributee Summary
  • Wrong petition form — using the Administration petition when there is a will, or vice versa
  • Missing Waiver and Consent forms from heirs who were eligible to sign
  • Incomplete family tree — leaving out a deceased child's children (grandchildren of decedent)
  • Filing in the wrong county — must be the county where the decedent was domiciled
  • Wrong filing fee — Surrogate's Court fees are based on estate value, not flat rate

Path 1: Probate — Step by Step

Probate is the court process of admitting a will as valid and authorizing the executor to act. In New York this happens in the Surrogate's Court of the county where the deceased person lived.

1
Secure the original will immediately

You must have the physical original. Not a copy, not a scan — the original signed document. If someone else has it, you may need to demand it or get a court order. Do not proceed to file until you have it.

2
Get a certified death certificate

Order from the vital records office in the state where the person died. You'll need at least 2–3 certified copies. Photocopies are not accepted.

3
Identify all distributees and prepare the Family Tree Exhibit

List every heir — spouse, children, grandchildren of deceased children, parents, siblings as applicable. This document must be complete. Missing an heir is one of the most common reasons probate petitions are rejected or delayed.

4
Get Waiver and Consent forms signed by cooperating heirs

Each adult heir who agrees to the proceeding signs a Waiver and Consent. This allows the case to move forward without formal citation (notice). Each heir signs their own copy before a notary.

5
File the Petition for Probate at Surrogate's Court

Bring the original will, certified death certificate, completed petition, family tree exhibit, and signed waivers. Pay the filing fee (based on estate value — ask the clerk for the current schedule).

6
Court issues Letters Testamentary

Once the judge approves the petition, you receive Letters Testamentary — the legal document giving you authority to act as executor. You can now collect assets, pay debts, and distribute the estate.

Path 2: Administration — Step by Step

When someone dies without a will, no executor is named. A qualified family member petitions for Letters of Administration — court authority to handle the estate.

1
Confirm there is truly no will

Before filing for administration, conduct a reasonable search: check the deceased's papers, safe deposit box, and the Surrogate's Court records in the county where they lived. If a will surfaces later after you've been appointed, it complicates everything.

2
Determine your priority to file

New York law (SCPA §1001) sets a priority order for who can be appointed administrator: spouse first, then children, then grandchildren, then parents, then siblings, then other distributees. If you have equal priority with another family member, you may need their consent or a resolution with them first.

3
Prepare the complete distributee list

Same as probate — every heir must be identified in the Family Tree Exhibit. The court uses this to determine who must be notified and who has rights to the estate.

4
Collect Waiver and Consent from other heirs

Heirs with equal or higher priority than you must sign Waiver and Consent forms agreeing to your appointment — or the court will need to issue citations and hold a hearing.

5
File the Petition for Letters of Administration

Bring the certified death certificate, completed petition, family tree exhibit, waivers, and filing fee to Surrogate's Court in the county where the decedent lived.

6
Court issues Letters of Administration

Once approved, you receive Letters of Administration — your legal authority to collect assets, pay debts, and distribute the estate according to New York's intestacy law.

Path 3: Small Estate / Voluntary Administration

If the estate qualifies — personal property under $50,000, no real estate solely in the decedent's name, no potential lawsuit — the Voluntary Administration process under SCPA §1310 is faster and less expensive than full probate or administration.

You file an Affidavit in Relation to Voluntary Administration along with the death certificate and a simplified family tree. The court processing time is typically shorter and the filing fee is lower.

Important: Even if the numbers seem to qualify, real estate in the decedent's name alone or a possible lawsuit typically disqualifies the small estate route. Use the free inheritance tool to confirm before filing.

Which County Do You File In?

You file in the Surrogate's Court of the county where the deceased person was domiciled (their primary residence) at the time of death. Not where they owned property, not where they died — where they lived.

If the decedent lived in Brooklyn, file in Kings County Surrogate's Court. If they lived in White Plains, file in Westchester County Surrogate's Court.
